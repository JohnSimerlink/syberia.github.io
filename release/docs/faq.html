Frequently Asked Questions

What about ♫Big Data♫?

"Programs must be written for people to read, and only incidentally for machines to execute."
—Hal Abelson

What about it? As my thesis advisor said "understanding mathematics is not about complex abstract ideas, but about understanding simple things well." If you worry the size of your data is the problem, you are probably not taking the correct approach to a solution. At Avant, we regularly use Syberia to turn our problems into embarrassingly parallel work units or use Syberia to call out to distributed C++ code that has already solved the problem.

Syberia acts as a rapid syntactic scaffolding tool to avoid being slowed down by compiling JAR files or submitting DAGs to distributed job runners: debugging those things will be pretty painful even with a stack trace. It's much nicer staying in a responsive REPL that keeps you feeling fresh and iterating quickly.

While we are not ready to share our solutions to these problems, we promise that putting syntax first, like a mathematician, will lead to great things. There is a good reason why the definitions are hard and the theorems are easy. Solve the problem of fitting and daily re-deploying thousands of feature engineering pipelines and ML models in response to radically different academic and business questions with a succint high-level parametrization of the full modeling process while maintaining full end-to-end testability, modularity, continuous integration, and zero-code deployment over every model you have ever experimentally created whilst avoiding nasty feedback loops over your customer distribution due to lack of consideration of IID violation and systematic biasing, and we promise that ♫Big Data♫ will be an easy final step.

Otherwise we'll give you your money back.



How do I deploy my model?

This is a question for your devops engineer. While we are not ready to share our solutions to these problems, there are plenty of approaches already. The Syberia modeling engine builds you a model object that is easily serializable. If you can park it on some persistent storage and read it in dynamically, just write a 10-line web server that takes a JSON hash of the rawest, messiest production data you can give it, turns it into a 1-row data.frame (don't forget to let mungebits do all the hard munging work to avoid hardcoding in aspects of the feature engineering process), and calls

    
send_back_json_response(list(score = model$predict(one_row_of_data)))

You can use microserver, Rserve, or bake the R run-time into a simple C server. The sky is the limit. Docker might help.

But R is slow

That's not a question. Syberia's original use case was to deploy very complicated topologies of models, with one feeding into another which ensembles with a third which finally feeds into a fourth, and deploying the solution without having to write a single extra line of code. If you can't tolerate a couple dozen milliseconds of latency, or a couple hundred for the more complex data science products, then I guess you have no recourse but to re-write your hard work in C.

Alternatively, you could transpile the current modeling engine DSL to C just-in-time. You'll get to keep your fast iteration speed but the recipe requires a few C gurus to maintain the mapping.
